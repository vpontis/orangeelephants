2009 BOPD SCA: Assignment: Period 3 - Botball Lessons Learned
 

What did you gain from the Botball experience?

      Our motley team consisted of casual lifelong roboticists, veteran Botball participants, hotshot computer software developers, dedicated documenters, and others who were less experienced but equally enthusiastic, and thus, just as productive. From this astounding diversity we derived our strength, but it was a challenge keeping everybody working in harmony with one another. Towards the beginning of season, one day a hardware person would build a mechanism, the next, another would take it apart and redesign it; we operated at cross purposes. But by the end, we had established effective communication between our team members – hardware and software and documentation – and were able to operate in parallel for the benefit of all. 

What did you gain from the documentation process?

      Most of all, by documenting, we realized the amount of viable ideas we discussed and rapidly forgot about. We made a habit of recording any conversations we had on strategy or design or point scoring as not to miss any details for documentation, and suddenly, we realized how much important information we had been otherwise rapidly forgetting. Furthermore, the hard deadlines motivated us not to procrastinate – a single Internet connection failure and we would be virtually out of the running to win the regional tournament for lack of documentation points. The work ethic we learned here will certainly persist throughout our academic career and hopefully, our lives. 

What surprised you most about your Botball experience?

      Our team was stunned by our collective enthusiasm for the task set before us. Generally, the “group projects” are dreaded for the lethargy they induce in the less motivated members, but here, perhaps because Botball is an optional and inherently fun activity (and are team members are all excellent academics), we had very little slacking and relatively consistent meeting attendance. And we worked extremely efficiently when present.

      Furthermore, we were consistently surprised by the brilliance of our fellow teammates: and every meeting revealed heretofore unseen skills in our coders, builders, and documenters, even though many of us have known one another for years. For example, one day in week two or three, Jacob and Minh – our main hardware designers –arrived at a meeting to find a new, extremely robust prototype of the LEGO Bot drive train sitting on the floor. We were perplexed: where could this have come from? Did a butterfly flap its wings somewhere across the world and cause a strange eddy in the air currents that, upon reaching San Diego entered our Botball workspace and blew the LEGO and Vex pieces into a perfect mechanism? After considering that hypothesis for a few moments, we decided it to be somewhat unlikely, so we asked the team what had happened. It turned out that Mark, one of our main software developers, had suddenly come upon great inspiration and worked late into the night carrying out his ideas. To this day, we use his superbly economical, astoundingly reliable driving base that he constructed on that inspired evening. 

What advice would you pass on to future Botball teams?

      Above all, we would advise future Botball teams to select their members based on willingness, dedication, and experience, not innate skill or intelligence, or academic renown. Although many people on our team possess the latter qualities, those do not necessarily bring about successful completion and refinement of a robot – that takes week after week, day after day – late evening after later evening – of focus, practice, and unquenchable enthusiasm.

      Also, do not feel like you have to include complicated mechanisms in your robots to win or succeed or be reliable. Instead, adhere to spiral model of development: rush to build something that works, test it extensively, then redesign and repeat the process. You will have enough trouble making it work reliably when Keeping It Simple. Besides, complexity will rapidly evolve on its own. That was our team’s problem last year, and our success in avoiding that pitfall this year is why we have thus far achieved the rather ambitious goals we have set. 
 

Other Notes:

       We learned that it was important to hardcode motion before integrating sensor feedback – initially, we attempted to include as many cameras and IR rangefinders and other detection devices as possible (“More sensor input is better, right?” we erroneously reasoned). Instead, it is better to progress as far as possible without sensor only add them when absolutely necessary. Doing otherwise actually leads to less reliable code: it is likely that somewhere, a sensor value will end up untripped due to the unpredictability of arena conditions. Instead, only rely on the most consistent and most important feedback.

      We also learned that metal should be used whenever it can be, and certainly on all weight-bearing mechanisms. LEGO pieces are simply not dependable enough: a 9/10 success rate is not acceptable if victory is the goal. That is what prompted the redesign of our LEGO robot.
